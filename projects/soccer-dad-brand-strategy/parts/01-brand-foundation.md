# Part 1 — Brand Foundation
**Agent**: Soccer Dad Brand Strategist | **Wave**: 1 | **Status**: Locked

---

## 1. Brand Name

**SIDELINE PAPI**

"Sideline" is a universally understood soccer and sports word — it places you physically at the field without requiring explanation. "Papi" is not a translation of "dad." It is a cultural identity marker. It carries warmth, authority, humor, and belonging in a single word that lands in both English and Spanish without needing code-switching. The name describes who the brand *is*, not what it does.

Fits on a hat brim in six characters of breathing room. Handle @SidelinePapi is clean, memorable, and searchable.

**Why the others were eliminated:**
- **Touchline Dads** — "Touchline" signals fan-culture over family-culture and skews Anglo in its register, undercutting the cultural mission from the name up.
- **The 90th Minute** — Names a moment, not a person; reads as a performance brand or media property rather than a lifestyle brand built around dads.
- **Pitch Dads Co.** — "Co." doing too much heavy lifting; "Pitch Dads" without the suffix is generic enough to belong to anyone, which means it belongs to no one.
- **Primera Fila** — Beautiful phrase, strong cultural resonance, but fails the English-dominant bilingual test and positions the brand as a spectator identity rather than a participant identity.

---

## 2. Tagline

**"Every Saturday, we show up."**

This line operates on three levels simultaneously: description of literal behavior (weekend games), declaration of community identity (we, collectively), and emotional statement about what kind of father this person chooses to be — present, consistent, accountable.

*Runners-up (available for campaign use):*
- "Built for the dad on the sideline." — competent but positions the brand as product utility, not cultural statement
- "Your kids know you're there." — emotionally resonant but sentimental in a way that narrows the brand's range

---

## 3. Positioning Statement

**Formal:**
"For dads and dad figures who spend their weekends at youth soccer fields — especially in bilingual and bicultural Latino communities — Sideline Papi is the lifestyle and apparel brand that makes you feel seen, celebrated, and connected to a community of fathers who show up the same way you do, because this brand was built from the sideline out, not from a boardroom in."

**Plain-English (how the founder tells it at the field):**
"I was tired of brands that sold gear to soccer parents but didn't actually know us. They made stuff for the player or for the coach. Nobody was making something for the guy who's out here every Saturday at seven in the morning with the folding chair and the coffee and the kids' snacks in a bag on his shoulder, cheering in two languages and trying not to lose his voice before halftime. Sideline Papi is for that guy. It's gear he's actually proud to wear, and it's a community where he doesn't have to explain himself — because everybody around him already gets it."

## 4. Brand Pillars

### Pillar 1: Show Up Culture
**Definition:** Presence — at the field, in the community, in the content — is the brand's core value.

**DO:** Feature real dads with real attendance records — a dad who has been at every game for four years, a dad who drove three hours for a tournament — and tell those stories with their names and their kids' names and their club colors.

**DON'T:** Never use stock photography of vague, undefined "sports parents." If there is no real story to tell yet, tell nothing until there is.

---

### Pillar 2: Bilingual by Default
**Definition:** The brand does not translate — it lives comfortably in both English and Spanish simultaneously, treating bilingual communication as natural expression, not a marketing accommodation.

**DO:** Write product names, captions, and packaging copy that functions in both languages without a separate "Spanish version" — the original is already both.

**DON'T:** Never publish a Spanish caption as an afterthought addendum beneath an English-primary post with the note "también en español" — that structure signals that Spanish is the secondary language, which is a lie about who this community is.

---

### Pillar 3: Community Before Commerce
**Definition:** The brand builds relationships, gatherings, and trust before it builds a product catalog, and every commercial decision is tested against whether it strengthens or weakens the community that sustains it.

**DO:** Before the first merch drop, host a community moment — a free field event, a group chat, a local gathering — so that the first thing people associate with Sideline Papi is belonging, not purchasing.

**DON'T:** Never launch a limited-edition drop designed around artificial scarcity purely for hype; if scarcity exists, it should be a production reality explained honestly.

---

### Pillar 4: Unapologetically Dad
**Definition:** The brand leans into dad identity without apology, irony, or self-deprecation — being a present, committed, loud-on-the-sideline father is something worth celebrating at full volume.

**DO:** Write copy that takes the Saturday-morning dad seriously as an athlete of presence — his commitment, his logistics management, his emotional labor, his six-chair setup at 7 AM deserves the same respect a gear brand gives a midfielder.

**DON'T:** Never lean into "bad dad" humor — no jokes about dads who can't cook, dads who fall asleep, dads who don't know what they're doing — even if it tests well in focus groups.

---

## 5. Voice Guide

### Tone in 3 Adjectives

**Warm — but not soft.**
Speaks like someone who actually knows you, not like a brand performing familiarity. Not saccharine, doesn't over-compliment, doesn't pepper every sentence with exclamation points.

**Confident — but not chest-puffing.**
Knows who it is and does not need to announce it constantly. Does not compare itself to other brands, does not brag in a way that excludes.

**Playful — but not performative.**
Humor is genuine — the kind of laugh you get at the field between two dads who just watched the same chaotic play. Never a bit for engagement. No memes as substitute for substance.

---

### The Brand Sounds Like This (5 examples)

1. "Six AM. Cleats in the backseat. Coffee in your hand. You've been ready since Thursday. Sideline Papi."
2. "Your kid looked up at you in the third minute. You were already there. That's the whole thing."
3. "We don't make gear for the guy who shows up sometimes. We make it for the guy who hasn't missed one."
4. "Sábado es sagrado. See you at the field."
5. "You don't need a trophy. You've got a kid who knows your face in the crowd."

### The Brand Never Says This (5 corrections)

| Wrong | Right | What changed |
|---|---|---|
| "Hey soccer fam! Feeling pumped for the weekend? Drop a ⚽ if you're ready to CRUSH IT!" | "This weekend. Coffee, chairs, and your kid's name in your chest. See you there." | Removed hollow hype language; replaced with concrete image |
| "We're more than a brand — we're a MOVEMENT. Join the revolution!" | "We're a community of dads who don't miss. That's it. Come be part of it." | Stripped inflated corporate activism language |
| "Para nuestros amigos hispanohablantes — also available en español!" | Caption exists in both registers from the first word, written as one piece | Eliminated patronizing separation that makes Spanish an add-on |
| "As dads, we all know how hard it is to balance work, life, family..." | "You rearranged your whole Saturday. Again. And you'd do it again tomorrow." | Cut therapy-speak; replaced with specific true statement |
| "Limited drop — only 50 units — don't sleep or you'll miss out forever!!!" | "First run is small. We made what we could stand behind. If you're in, you're in." | Replaced scarcity manipulation with honest production reality |

---

### Spanglish Policy

**Rule 1:** Spanglish appears when one language genuinely does the job better — not to signal diversity. "Sábado es sagrado" works as a complete thought. Use the Spanish because it is the better sentence, not as decoration.

**Rule 2:** Never use Spanglish as a translation device mid-sentence. If you are writing a Spanish word to explain an English concept, you have written neither language correctly.

**Rule 3:** The brand never code-switches to signal authenticity — it code-blends because that is how the community actually speaks.

## 6. Go-to-Market Launch Sequence

### Week 1 — Community Foundation (No Public Presence)
The founder identifies 12–15 real soccer dads — specifically from bilingual and bicultural Latino communities — and has individual conversations with them at the field, in parking lots, over coffee. Not a focus group. A relationship. These become the founding community members.

**This happens first because:** The brand's entire positioning — community before commerce — cannot be retroactive. If the first action is a product or social post, the brand has already told the truth about what it actually prioritizes, and that truth is the wrong one.

### Week 2 — First Public-Facing Action
One piece of content: a portrait. One real dad, photographed at his kid's field, with a caption written in his words about why he shows up. No logo watermark dominating the image. No call to action. No link in bio.

**Why:** Every subsequent piece of content and commerce is read through the lens of the first thing the brand ever said publicly. If the first thing is a hat, the brand is a hat company. If the first thing is a father's story, the brand is a community.

### Full 90-Day Sequence

| Week | Milestone | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Private community conversations (12–15 founding dads) | Community before commerce; no public presence yet |
| 2 | @SidelinePapi goes live; founding dad portrait drops | First impression: people over product |
| 3–4 | Content cadence launches (one story + one Spanglish caption/week) | Build without selling |
| 6 | First local chapter event — free field gathering, founding members only | Physical community before digital community becomes a substitute |
| 10 | First merch drop (The Founding Drop / La Primera Bajada) | Commerce follows because belonging is real |

### One Thing NOT to Do First
**Do NOT launch with the product.** This community — bilingual, bicultural, Latino youth soccer families — has a finely calibrated radar for extraction. They have watched their culture get aestheticized and sold back to them by brands that used their imagery without their involvement. A Sideline Papi that leads with "buy this hat" is indistinguishable from that pattern on day one. Launch with belonging. The commerce follows.
