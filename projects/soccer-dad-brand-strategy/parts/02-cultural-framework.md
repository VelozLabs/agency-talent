## 1. NAME FILTER — Cultural Evaluation

### Ranked Preference: 1st to 5th

---

**1st — Sideline Papi**

*Papi* is not a Spanish loan word dropped into English for flavor — it is a living, affectionate term used daily in bilingual households, and it carries warmth, authority, and humor simultaneously. This name centers the bicultural dad as the subject, not as a demographic, and the word *Sideline* grounds it in a shared soccer experience that crosses language lines without requiring translation.

The risk of appropriation is low because *Papi* is self-identifying language — it is the word the community uses for itself, not a word an outsider chose to signal cultural alignment. No one outside this community would name their brand *Sideline Papi* with a straight face, which is exactly why it works.

---

**2nd — Primera Fila**

*Primera Fila* (First Row, or "front row") is an entirely Spanish-language name, and that is both its strength and its strategic question. It centers Spanish-dominant families completely, signals that this brand is not asking Latino parents to meet English halfway, and carries an aspirational register — the front row is where you put yourself when you are fully committed to your kid.

The risk here is narrowing the addressable audience at the brand name level before any content has a chance to do the work. It is the correct name if the brand is willing to own a Spanish-first identity all the way through; it is a liability if the brand later hedges into English-dominant content and the name becomes a costume rather than a commitment.

---

**3rd — The 90th Minute**

This name is soccer-fluent and emotionally resonant across language lines — every parent who has stood at a youth game knows the feeling of the 90th minute regardless of whether they speak Spanish or English. It does not pretend to be bilingual, which is its honesty; it is a universal soccer phrase that does not appropriate anything.

The cultural limitation is that it does not signal bicultural identity at all — it is a name that could belong to a coaching app, a match recap newsletter, or an equipment brand. It requires the content and voice to carry all of the cultural weight the name does not, which is a harder marketing lift and makes it a weaker choice for a brand whose differentiator is the bilingual experience.

---

**4th — Touchline Dads**

*Touchline Dads* is clear, correct, and completely culturally neutral — which is the problem. The word *touchline* is a British-English soccer term that is more common among club soccer vocabularies than it is in the actual conversation of a Latino dad yelling from the sideline in Los Angeles or Houston. It reads as the vocabulary of the American soccer establishment, not the community this brand exists to serve.

It is not a name that risks appropriation, but it risks something equally damaging for this specific brand: it risks making the Latino audience feel like a targeted marketing segment within a brand built for someone else. I would not endorse this name for this brand concept.

---

**5th — Pitch Dads Co.**

This name has the lowest cultural value of the five. The word *Pitch* carries the same British-English soccer register problem as *Touchline*, and the addition of *Co.* turns it into a generic lifestyle brand suffix that signals nothing culturally specific. The combination reads like a brand name generated to fill a gap rather than a name that emerged from community language.

*Pitch Dads Co.* would require the brand to do enormous cultural work in every other asset to overcome a name that actively pulls in the wrong direction. I would not endorse this name, and I flag it as a cultural non-starter for a brand positioning around authentic Latino soccer family identity.

## 2. LANGUAGE MIX DECISION

### Instagram: 50% Spanglish / 30% English-dominant / 20% Spanish-dominant

Instagram content lives or dies on the feed and the relationship between a post and its existing followers. The audience here has already opted in — they followed the account because something resonated. This means the brand can and should speak the way the community actually speaks, which is Spanglish. Half of all posts should be written in genuine mid-sentence code-switching because that is the authentic register of a bilingual dad sending a text to another bilingual dad.

The 30% English-dominant slice covers content designed for discovery — a Reel about youth soccer culture that will be shared to people who do not yet know the brand, a collab post, a product drop. These posts are English-accessible but never English-only; they should always contain at least one Spanish or Spanglish phrase in the caption. The 20% Spanish-dominant posts are the brand's commitment signal — content that says *this house is your house* to the Spanish-first parent who navigates most of American life in translation. Stories and close-friends content can skew heavier Spanish.

### TikTok: 60% English-accessible Spanglish / 25% English-dominant / 15% Spanish-dominant

TikTok's ForYou Page algorithm distributes content to people who have never seen the account before, based on engagement signals and watch time rather than follower relationships. This structural reality requires a different calculus than Instagram. A fully Spanish-dominant video will have strong engagement from the core community but will be throttled by the algorithm before it reaches the broader bicultural audience the brand needs to grow. The brand cannot afford to optimize only for the already-converted on a platform where discovery is the primary growth mechanism.

English-accessible Spanglish is the TikTok sweet spot: video content that sounds natural and bicultural in the audio — a dad saying *"Oye, el niño tiene everything, everything, bro"* — while remaining emotionally intelligible to a monolingual English speaker who watches to the end because the moment is funny, real, or recognizable. Captions and on-screen text should default to English with Spanglish accents. The 15% fully Spanish-dominant content is reserved for cultural moments (Hispanic Heritage Month, Father's Day reels, Día de los Muertos tributes) where the brand's cultural identity is the content itself.

### Email (The Collective): Segmented by preference, default to Spanglish subject lines

**Subject lines**: Default to Spanglish for all subscribers, because the subject line is a culture signal before it is a marketing message. Examples: *"Ya empezó la temporada. ¿Estás listo?"* or *"El drop is here, Papi."* These subject lines tell the reader within three words whether this email is for them.

**Body content**: Bilingual subscribers receive Spanglish body copy — natural switching within paragraphs, not parallel columns or translated footnotes. English-dominant subscribers (captured at signup) receive English-primary body with Spanglish phrases retained intact and not explained or translated; the brand does not apologize for its voice, but it writes accessibly.

**Language preference capture**: At newsletter signup, the brand presents a single low-friction question — not a dropdown labeled "Select your language" but a choice phrased in the brand voice: *"¿Cómo hablamos? / How do we talk?"* with two options: **"Spanglish, always"** and **"English-primary."** No Spanish-only option at signup — if a subscriber replies or engages exclusively in Spanish, customer service responds in Spanish, and the CRM tags them for Spanish-dominant sends. Language preference is inferred through behavior, not forced through bureaucratic classification.

### Structural Decision: ONE bilingual account

**Recommendation: Maintain one bilingual account across all channels. Do not split into two.**

The business rationale is brand integrity, not operational convenience. This brand's differentiator is the bilingual, bicultural experience as a unified identity — not two separate communities that happen to like soccer. Splitting into an English account and a Spanish account would structurally contradict the brand's core argument, which is that the bicultural soccer dad does not split himself into two people depending on the language of the room. A Spanish-only account also risks becoming a ghetto rather than a home — a secondary account with lower reach, lower production investment, and the implicit message that the Spanish-speaking parent deserves a separate (smaller) experience. One account, many voices, the whole community in the same room.

## 3. SPANGLISH AUTHENTICITY STANDARD

### 10 Authentic Brand Voice Phrases

These are phrases a bilingual soccer dad would actually say, text, or caption — not phrases a marketing team constructed to sound bilingual.

1. *"El nene scored twice today. TWICE. I don't know what they've been feeding him pero he's different right now."*

2. *"Tres horas de drive para un empate. Pero qué le vas a hacer — that's the game, bro."*

3. *"Oye, they called offsides on that? Eso no fue nada, ref. NOTHING."*

4. *"My kid's coach doesn't speak Spanish pero somehow we always understand each other. Fútbol es el idioma universal, I swear."*

5. *"Temporada empieza el sábado. Manos arriba si you've already washed the jersey four times."*

6. *"No hay nada like seeing your kid get back up after a hard foul and just keep running. That's the whole thing right there, hermano."*

7. *"Bro, I packed tamales for the tournament and by the end of warm-ups había nada left. Somos populares."*

8. *"She's 11 years old and she's already got more composure on the ball than I ever did. Qué orgullo, man. Qué orgullo."*

9. *"El partido was at 7am. 7AM. And I was there with my café and my folding chair like — this is my life and I love it."*

10. *"They grow up fast, bro. Antes I was carrying him to the field. Now I can barely keep up watching him. Disfrútenlos."*

---

### 10 Inauthentic Phrases — and Why Each Fails

1. *"Somos the soccer family!"*
**Fails because**: *Somos* inserted at the front is decorative Spanish, not code-switching — it is English syntax with a Spanish opening word bolted on as a signal. Real code-switching does not perform itself.

2. *"This jersey is muy fuego."*
**Fails because**: *Muy* before an English slang word is the exact construction bilingual people mock. It is translation-brain Spanish applied to English — no bilingual speaker does this naturally.

3. *"¡Hola, Soccer Familia!"*
**Fails because**: *Familia* instead of *family* when the rest of the sentence is English is hollow — it is using Spanish only on the warm, soft noun to signal inclusivity while keeping everything else English. It is Taco Bell grammar.

4. *"We celebrate our Latino cultura every game day."*
**Fails because**: Swapping *culture* for *cultura* in an otherwise English sentence, especially around the word *celebrate*, is the exact register of a corporate diversity campaign. The word *cultura* here is a prop, not a voice.

5. *"Los dads are in the house!"*
**Fails because**: Applying a Spanish article (*Los*) to the English noun *dads* is a construction that does not occur in natural bilingual speech — it is linguistic costuming, and the community will read it immediately as outsider code.

6. *"Whether you speak español or English, we're for you."*
**Fails because**: This is an English inclusion statement that uses *español* as a token. It signals that the default is English and Spanish is the thing being accommodated, which is the opposite of this brand's positioning.

7. *"Kick. Pass. Golazo. This is our game."*
**Fails because**: Inserting *Golazo* as the emotional peak of an otherwise English-language list is exactly how a brand that does not actually live in the bilingual community would use Spanish — as a burst of flavor on top of an English structure.

8. *"¡El partido is about to commence!"*
**Fails because**: *Commence* in this context is hypercorrect formal English that no soccer dad — in any language — would ever say. This fails on register, not just language.

9. *"Para our padres out there, Happy Father's Day."*
**Fails because**: *Para* and *padres* are used together, which is doubly performative Spanish — and then the pivot to *Happy Father's Day* does not switch, it retreats. No code-switch earned.

10. *"We're bringing el sabor to the pitch."*
**Fails because**: *El sabor* (the flavor) used to describe a Latino brand in an English sentence is cultural shorthand that reduces the community to a seasoning. It is othering dressed as celebration.

---

### 3 Rules for Natural Code-Switching

**Rule 1: Switch at the emotional seam, not the grammatical boundary.**
Code-switching happens in real speech when the available word in one language better captures the feeling — not when a sentence structurally "allows" it. The switch should happen where the emotion peaks or shifts, not where it is grammatically convenient. *"She scored and I— bro, se me aguaron los ojos right there on the sideline"* switches because the Spanish phrase for tearing up (*se me aguaron los ojos*) carries an intimacy the English idiom does not.

**Rule 2: Never translate what you just said.**
If the copy switches language and then restates the same idea in the other language, it is translation, not code-switching, and it reads as either condescending or performative. Write for the bilingual reader who does not need the explanation. Trust the audience. *"Qué orgullo — what a proud moment for us all"* is a translation. *"Qué orgullo, bro. That kid's going to remember this forever"* is a switch.

**Rule 3: Respect the grammar of each language inside the switch.**
When Spanish appears in a sentence, it follows Spanish grammar. When English appears, it follows English grammar. Do not apply English structure to Spanish phrases or vice versa. *"I'm so excitado"* is not code-switching — it is an English word with a Spanish suffix, which is a different (and dismissible) construction. *"Estoy excited for the season"* is a legitimate switch: the Spanish clause is grammatically Spanish, and *excited* is borrowed the way bilingual speakers actually borrow content words.

---

### The Emotional Register Rule

**Spanish appears at moments of depth, intimacy, and emotion. English dominates in the practical, the humorous, and the logistical. Never reverse this.**

The community uses Spanish when the feeling is too important to risk losing in translation, and English when speed, humor, or shared practical information is the priority. The brand must honor this pattern, not invent its own.

**Example 1 (Spanish for depth):** A Father's Day post that closes with *"Que sepan que los estamos viendo. Siempre."* — the brand does not translate this. The bilingual reader knows exactly what it means, and the weight of those words in Spanish is the point.

**Example 2 (English for humor/logistics):** A post about tournament parking logistics: *"Seven fields, one parking lot, thirty-five minutes before kickoff. God bless us."* — this is English because the humor is dry, the situation is universal, and the joke lands faster in English. A Spanish version would not improve it; it would slow it down.

## 5. REPRESENTATION STANDARDS & RED LINES

### Photography Standards

**Who must appear and in what contexts:**

Latino fathers — specifically, a range of phenotypes reflecting the actual diversity of the pan-Latino community, including Afro-Latino men — must appear as the primary subjects in all lifestyle content. They appear on sidelines, in parking lots, in the car, in tournament bleachers, holding coffee, holding soccer balls, holding their kids. They are never in the background of content where a white father is centered in the foreground.

Children of color must appear in the majority of action and field-level content. Children of multiple ethnic backgrounds appear together naturally — as teammates, not as a posed diversity statement. The composition must look like a real youth soccer team in Los Angeles, Houston, Chicago, or Miami, not like a brochure where children of different backgrounds are positioned in equal symmetry.

Mothers appear regularly in supporting and equal roles — never only in domestic-adjacent contexts. A soccer mom appears on the sideline, in the team photo, driving the carpool, coaching, and celebrating. If a mother appears, she is not set decoration for the dad's story.

**Proportion guideline:**

A minimum of 60% of individuals depicted across all brand photography should visibly be Latino or of Latin American descent. No single shoot may drop below 50% Latino representation. Afro-Latino individuals must be intentionally included in casting — they are not an afterthought or a token; they are the community. All casting calls must specify: *"We are seeking subjects who reflect the Latino soccer community. Afro-Latino, Indigenous-featured, and mixed-heritage individuals are actively sought."*

**What is prohibited in brand photography:**

- The "translator dad" shot: a Latino father looking confused or uncertain while a white adult (coach, official, administrator) explains something to him. This visual encodes a power dynamic that the brand explicitly rejects.
- All-white team photos used as primary creative. If stock photography is ever used as a placeholder, it may not feature an all-white or majority-white lineup.
- Children in national-flag-themed gear used as the sole marker of Latino identity. A Mexican flag jersey is not a substitute for authentic storytelling.
- Poverty aesthetics: cracked concrete fields, worn-out gear presented as "grit content," and similar visual frames that use hardship as cultural authenticity. The community's excellence is the story, not their adversity.
- Fathers in passive, peripheral roles while white coaches or officials occupy the active center of the frame.
- The "exotic food as cultural marker" visual — no shots of tamales, pupusas, or other traditional foods staged as if they are curiosities rather than a normal part of a family's game-day routine.

### The 5 Cultural Red Lines

**Red Line 1: The brand will never use Spanish as decoration on an English-first product.**

Crossing it looks like: A hoodie that says *"Familia"* in large letters on the front while every other piece of copy — hang tags, website, email, social — is entirely in English with no genuine bilingual identity. The Spanish word on the garment exists to signal Latino appeal while the brand itself does not live in that community.

---

**Red Line 2: The brand will never reduce national or ethnic diversity to a single Latino nationality.**

Crossing it looks like: A Hispanic Heritage Month campaign that features Mexican flags, lucha libre imagery, Día de los Muertos altars, and mariachi references as the sole representation of "Latino culture," while Guatemalan, Salvadoran, Colombian, Dominican, Puerto Rican, Peruvian, and other families in the community see nothing of themselves.

---

**Red Line 3: The brand will never use immigrant struggle, documentation status, or economic hardship as an emotional marketing device.**

Crossing it looks like: A Father's Day video that opens with a man crossing the border or working a construction job before cutting to him on the soccer sideline, framing the narrative as "immigrant makes good." The community's soccer life is not the reward at the end of a hardship story. This framing, no matter how emotionally produced, is exploitative.

---

**Red Line 4: The brand will never outsource bilingual voice to translation software or non-bilingual copywriters without community review.**

Crossing it looks like: A campaign caption that reads *"¡El partido comienza ahora! The match begins now — don't miss out on our exclusive collection!"* — a sentence that is not Spanglish, is not code-switching, and is not a voice. It is an English marketing sentence run through a translator and stapled to a Spanish opener. Any copy containing Spanish must be reviewed by a fluent bilingual speaker before publication.

---

**Red Line 5: The brand will never activate a cultural moment it has not earned the standing to address.**

Crossing it looks like: The brand — which has published no content about Indigenous communities, has no Indigenous representation in its photography, and has made no community relationships — produces a Día de los Muertos campaign because the moment is "on the cultural calendar." Cultural moments are activations the community invites you into, not content opportunities the brand claims because they fall on a calendar. If the brand does not have authentic relationship and standing in a specific cultural moment, the activation rating is LIGHT or NONE, not MAJOR.

---

Section 4 care flags on Hispanic Heritage Month, Día de los Muertos, and Copa América nationality balance require a designated internal cultural review process to be established before the first activation.*
