## CONTENT STRATEGY — SIDELINE PAPI

---

## 1. CONTENT PILLARS

---

### Pillar 1: The Saturday Morning Religion

This pillar lives in the before — the pre-dawn alarm, the packed cooler, the backseat conversation on the way to the field. Sideline Papi captures these moments with the reverence they deserve, not as hustle-culture fuel but as proof that showing up is its own reward, every single week.

**Post Concepts:**
1. **"It's 6:47am and I've already lost a shin guard"** — a real-time, one-take video shot in a kitchen or garage while a dad is scrambling before the first game of the day. No voiceover polish. Just the chaos, narrated live.
2. **"The pre-game talk I gave my kid this morning vs. the one I meant to give"** — a two-part format. Dad recounts the motivational speech he rehearsed in the shower versus what actually came out of his mouth in the parking lot.
3. **"Counting the years I have left on this sideline"** — a slower, quieter post. Dad faces camera at the field before kickoff. His kid is 9. He does the math out loud. No music. No captions needed.

**Primary Platform:** Instagram Reels — the ritual format rewards the emotional buildup that Reels can sustain over 45–90 seconds in a way that TikTok's faster scroll does not.

---

### Pillar 2: La Cultura en la Cancha

This pillar is about the fullness of what these dads bring to the sideline — the Spanish instructions shouted across the field, the Dominican or Mexican or Salvadoran coaching philosophy that no UEFA badge ever certified. The voice here is proud without being a performance of pride.

**Post Concepts:**
1. **"Instructions my dad gave me vs. instructions I give my son (same words, thirty years apart)"** — a dad recites the exact phrases he heard growing up — "¡Mueve los pies!" "¡Con ganas!" — and then we cut to him saying them this past Saturday. Same voice, different generation.
2. **"What it feels like to be the only Spanish-speaking dad on the sideline"** — quiet, direct to camera. No punchline. Just the experience of code-switching mid-cheer because you want your kid to hear you in the language that means something.
3. **"The snack my abuela would have brought vs. what I actually brought"** — humor and love in one shot. Dad holds up a Ziploc of orange slices next to a photo of what his grandmother would have arrived with: a full spread. The cultural richness of how we feed each other is the point.

**Primary Platform:** Instagram — the cultural depth and bilingual emotional weight lands better with an audience that scrolls with intent; TikTok gets the lighter version of this pillar.

---

### Pillar 3: Dads Who Coach (And Dads Who Should Not)

This pillar is about the sideline behavior that every youth soccer parent recognizes — the over-coaching, the unsolicited advice, the dad who is definitely not the coach but has a lot of thoughts. Sideline Papi holds the mirror up without cruelty, because the point is self-recognition, not mockery.

**Post Concepts:**
1. **"Stages of watching your kid miss a wide-open goal"** — a reaction video, no edits, shot from the actual sideline. Dad's face goes through acceptance, grief, and re-acceptance in real time. The comments write themselves.
2. **"What I'm shouting at the field vs. what the coach actually asked me to do (which was nothing)"** — split concept, deadpan delivery. Dad lists the four specific pieces of tactical advice he gave from fifteen yards away. Then reads back the email from the coach asking parents not to coach.
3. **"Rating my own sideline behavior from last season, 1 to 10"** — honest self-assessment. Dad gives himself a 6. Explains the deduction. Admits the incident at the Riverside tournament. This is the accountability content that makes people tag their co-parent immediately.

**Primary Platform:** TikTok — humor and self-recognition content performs faster and broader on TikTok, where the comedy beat rewards the quick hook and the comment section becomes part of the content.

---

### Pillar 4: These Kids, Man

This pillar zooms in on the actual children — not as props, not as inspiration content, but as real people who are interesting and funny and occasionally profound. The voice is an awed dad who is paying attention, not a parent performing pride for the algorithm.

**Post Concepts:**
1. **"My son's pre-game ritual, explained by him, in his exact words"** — kid faces the camera and describes what he does before every match. No coaching, no re-takes. Whatever comes out is the post.
2. **"The thing she said on the way home that I'll never forget"** — a single quote, black screen, white text. Something a real kid actually said after a game. A line that hits because it is true, not because it was written to hit.
3. **"First season vs. this season"** — side-by-side phone footage. The early stuff is always perfect: shaky, far away, the wrong kid in frame. The recent footage shows actual growth. The dad narrates without nostalgia-bait. Just: here is what I saw.

**Primary Platform:** Instagram — this pillar builds the emotional core of the community and performs best where followers are repeat visitors who have built relationship with the brand, not first-time discoverers.

---

### Pillar 5: The Sideline Papi Nation

This pillar is the community itself — other dads, other fields, other chapters of the same Saturday morning story. It expands the brand from one voice into a chorus without losing the original voice as the center. This is where portraits, local features, and community moments live.

**Post Concepts:**
1. **"Meet Orlando. He hasn't missed a game in six years."** — the founding dad portrait format that launches Week 2. One man, his field, his reason. Shot in natural light before or after a game. Caption is minimal. The face does the work.
2. **"Dads from six cities told me what they bring to every game. Here's what they all said."** — a gathered response post. Sideline Papi put a question to the community and collated answers. The post is a mosaic of what these men carry — literally and figuratively — to every Saturday.
3. **"Your chapter starts here"** — a call to local connection. Not a signup form energy. A dad facing camera saying: if you're in [city], I want to know who you are. The chapter event in Week 6 is the destination, but this is the invitation.

**Primary Platform:** Instagram — community portrait and expansion content needs the visual permanence of the grid and the depth of caption space; this pillar builds the archive that new followers land on.

*Hook:*
My son asked me why I always shout in Spanish when he plays. I didn't know how to answer him until I thought about it for three days.

*Body:*
It's not because I forget my English. It's because some things — the things that matter — I learned in Spanish first. ¡Con ganas! I didn't learn that from a coaching manual. I learned it from my father on a dirt field in Michoacán when I was seven and I didn't want to run anymore and he looked at me like running was the least I could do.

When I say ¡Con ganas! to my son, I am not translating a concept. I'm passing something down that has no translation. The wanting-with-your-whole-body. The showing-up-even-when.

He's starting to understand. Last week he came off the bench and looked back at me before he touched the field. I didn't say anything. Neither did he.

Eso es todo lo que necesito.

*Closer:*

My son, who is nine years old and apparently has the self-awareness I lack, came over at halftime and said, "Papi, you're doing the thing." I asked him what thing. He did an impression of me. I looked like a man trying to physically move players with my mind from twenty yards away.

I'm working on it. I'm not there yet but I am working on it. Coach René has my number. I think he's blocked it.

*Closer:*
Tag the dad on your team's sideline who needs to see this. You know who it is.

## 3. SAMPLE COPY — TIKTOK

---

**Concept 1: Relatable Sideline Moment**

**Concept Title:** "The Sideline Walk of Shame"

**Opening Hook (first 3 seconds):**
Text overlay on screen: *"The referee just made eye contact with me."*
Spoken over it: "Oh no."

**Story Arc:**

*Setup:* Dad is on the sideline, good mood, early in the game. His kid's team is down 1–0 but it's fine, it's early. He explains this to the camera in quick cuts between glances at the field. He has not said anything inappropriate. He is being very good today. He wants that acknowledged.

*Escalation/Turn:* His kid's teammate takes a touch out of bounds on what was clearly — clearly — a ball that was still in play. Dad says something. It's one word. It might have been two words. The referee stops the game, turns, and finds him in a crowd of forty people instantly, like a heat-seeking missile, like this man has Dad-shaped radar. The whistle. The look. The point.

*Landing:* Dad does the walk. Twelve feet backward, hands up, nodding like he agrees with everything, like this is all completely fair, like he wasn't just right. His son is covering his face with his jersey. Cut to: the parking lot afterward. His kid won't walk next to him. "We won 3–1." Long pause. "Worth it."

---

**Concept 2: Bilingual / Cultural Moment**

**Concept Title:** "Things That Don't Translate (And Aren't Supposed To)"

**Opening Hook (first 3 seconds):**
Spoken directly to camera, no text: "There are things I say to my kids at the field that I can't explain in English. And I've stopped trying."

**Story Arc:**

*Setup:* Dad sits in his car after a game, still in his camp chair posture, talking to camera. He's going to read a list. These are real phrases — things he shouts, things his own father shouted — and he's going to try, once, to translate each one. Then explain why the translation doesn't work.

*Escalation/Turn:* "¡Échale ganas!" He tries: "Give it effort?" He shakes his head. That's not it. Ganas isn't effort. Ganas is the thing underneath effort, the wanting that makes effort possible. You can't just say effort. "¡Así se hace!" "That's how it's done?" No. Así se hace has a pride in it, a recognition, like you just watched your kid do something that's been in the family for years and finally came out right. He goes through four phrases. Every translation attempt falls shorter than the last.

*Landing:* He looks at the camera. "My son is starting to say these things too. To his little brother. I don't know if he knows what he's passing down." Beat. "I do." Video ends. No music spike. Just the cut.

---

**Concept 3: Merch-Adjacent (Doesn't Feel Like an Ad)**

**Concept Title:** "What I Actually Wear to Every Game (And What It Says About Me)"

**Opening Hook (first 3 seconds):**
Dad holds up a faded, beat-up hoodie to camera.
Spoken: "This hoodie has been to forty-seven games. I counted."

**Story Arc:**

*Setup:* Dad explains the hoodie. It's not special. It's gray. It's from a 5K he didn't run well. But it's his game-day hoodie and it has been since his daughter's first U6 season and now she's U12 and the hoodie is still here. He talks about what sideline gear actually means — it's not fashion, it's armor, it's habit, it's identity. You're telling the field who you are when you walk up.

*Escalation/Turn:* He looks at other dads on his sideline. There's the guy in the full club kit who takes this very seriously. The dad in business casual who came from a meeting and never left. The grandfather in the panama hat who has never once been cold. Then he looks back at his hoodie. He says: "I've been thinking about what I actually want to represent when I walk up to that field. What I want my kid to see."

*Landing:* He sets the hoodie down on the passenger seat. "Something's coming. It's gonna be for us — the ones who actually show up." He doesn't say what. No link. No "drop date." Just the setup. Comments will ask. That's the point.

## 5. POSTING CADENCE

---

### Weekly Cadence

Youth soccer's real calendar: practices typically fall Tuesday and Thursday. Games are Saturday, with Sunday games for travel/tournament weekends. Monday is recovery. Wednesday is the midweek breath. Friday is the pregame anticipation window.

**Monday — Instagram — Morning (7–9am)**
*Content type: Reflection post / community throwback*
Monday morning is when the previous Saturday's game is still fresh and the emotional processing has had 36 hours to settle. This is the day for the quieter, more personal content — a quote from the weekend, a photo from the sideline, a one-paragraph caption about something that happened. Posted in the morning because parents are in transit and will catch it commuting. This is the post that gets saved, not shared.

**Tuesday — TikTok — Evening (7–9pm)**
*Content type: Humor / observational / sideline behavior*
Tuesday evening, after practice, parents are in the car or at home winding down. They have exactly the right energy for comedy — tired enough to not scroll with judgment, loose enough to laugh. This is the "Dads Who Coach" pillar's home. Post here with the quick hook and the self-recognition punchline.

**Thursday — Instagram Stories — Morning (6–8am)**
*Content type: Interactive story prompt (poll, question sticker, or slider)*
Thursday is the midweek community touchpoint. Parents have practice logistics on their minds, weekend games are getting real, and the community is primed to engage. Morning Stories catch the early scroll — before the workday — and the interactive format keeps it light and conversational heading into the weekend.

**Friday — TikTok + Instagram (cross-post with format adjustment) — Evening (6–8pm)**
*Content type: Pregame energy / cultural moment / "it's almost Saturday" content*
Friday evening is anticipation. Kids are prepping mentally; parents are packing bags, checking field locations, figuring out the snack rotation. Content on Friday should feel like a pregame — a little elevated, a little warm. This is the right window for La Cultura en la Cancha content or the bilingual emotional-weight posts. It lands when families are together in the early evening.

**Saturday — Instagram Stories (real-time) — Morning through midday (6am–1pm)**
*Content type: Real-time sideline dispatches — live Stories from the actual field*
Saturday is live. Not produced. The phone comes out at the parking lot, at the first warm-up drill, at halftime. Authentic, unpolished, in the moment. No captions crafted. Just the actual morning, documented as it happens. This is the heartbeat of the brand — if the content stops feeling real on Saturdays, nothing else holds.

**Sunday — Instagram — Afternoon (2–4pm)**
*Content type: Community content / Sideline Papi Nation portrait or feature*
Sunday afternoon, after the weekend's games, is the landing. People are home. The content can go deeper here — a dad portrait, a community spotlight, the bilingual Spanish-led post that rewards the readers who stay with it. This is the long-exhale content. It doesn't need a hook. It needs to be worth stopping for.

---

### Monthly Anchors

**1. The First-of-the-Month Dad Portrait**
Every month, on or near the first Saturday, Sideline Papi publishes one dad portrait — one real man, his field, his story. No production polish. Natural light, honest caption, Spanish or English depending on whose story it is. This becomes the archive. By month six, the grid is also a record of the community.

**2. The Sideline Confession**
Mid-month, every month: a post where Sideline Papi admits something. A sideline behavior. A parenting miss. A moment of overcooking it that everyone recognizes. This is the accountability content that keeps the brand from becoming aspirational in the wrong way. The community needs to know this is not a perfect-dad account.

**3. Community Roundup**
Near the last week of the month: a gathered-response post, built from comment answers, DMs, or Story responses collected all month. "You told me what phrase your dad used to shout. Here's what you said." The community becomes the content. The account becomes the platform, not the performer.

**4. The Saturday Count**
On the last Saturday of the month: a quiet, simple post. How many Saturdays have come and gone in this sport with your kid? Some dads have twelve left. Some have two hundred. The post doesn't dramatize this — it just holds the number up, lets each parent do their own math, and leaves them with the tagline. This one should always end the same way. Every Saturday, we show up.

---

### Pre-Drop Content Sequence: 7 Days Before First Merch Drop

**Day 7 (Monday) — Instagram — Morning**
No announcement. A post about what it means to wear something with intention on the sideline. The faded hoodie concept. The dad who's been wearing the same thing for six years. The gear is a habit, not a fashion choice. The comment section fills with people describing their own game-day uniform.

**Day 6 (Tuesday) — TikTok — Evening**
A video asking the audience directly: "What would you actually want it to say?" Not hinting at a drop — framing it as community input. The question feels genuine because it is genuine. The brand has been listening for ten weeks. This is the callback.

**Day 5 (Wednesday) — Instagram Stories — Morning**
A poll: "Sideline Papi gear — would you wear it to the field or to the grocery store?" Both answers are correct. The poll is a warmup.

**Day 4 (Thursday) — TikTok — Evening**
A video: "Here's what I wanted. Here's what I couldn't find anywhere." Dad talks about the gap in the market — not for content dads or influencer dads but for the real sideline dad who wants to rep something that actually reflects him. Still no product shown. The want is being named.

**Day 3 (Friday) — Instagram — Evening**
First visual hint. Not a product photo — a detail. Fabric texture, a corner of the design, the feel of the thing. Caption: "Diez semanas on the sideline. Something's ready." Nothing else.

**Day 2 (Saturday) — Instagram Stories — Live from the field**
That Saturday morning, real-time Stories. Someone on the sideline is wearing the unreleased piece. No callout. No arrow. Just: it's there, it exists, it's on a real dad at a real field. The audience will spot it.

**Day 1 (Sunday) — Instagram + TikTok — Afternoon**
The drop announcement. Not a countdown, not a hype reel. A clean post: the piece, the price, the link. Caption is three sentences. "This is for the dad who shows up every Saturday. We made it for the sideline, not the 'gram. It's here." TikTok version is a 45-second video — the field, the dad, the piece on his back as he watches his kid play. No voice-over. Just the moment, and then the link in bio.

### Seasonal Shifts

**Tournament Season (High-Frequency Game Weekends):**
During tournament season, when a single weekend can mean three or four games across two days, the Saturday and Sunday real-time Story content expands to become the backbone of the week — planned posts compress to Monday and Friday only, and the energy of the account leans fully live, raw, and field-adjacent, because that is where the community is and that is what they recognize.

**Off-Season:**
During the off-season, the weekly cadence shifts from game-day urgency to memory and anticipation — the content slows to four posts per week rather than six, leans into the dad portrait and community roundup pillars, and begins planting the narrative seeds (new seasons, new goals, kids aging up) that make the return to Saturdays feel earned rather than sudden.

## 6. HASHTAG STRATEGY

---

### Primary Hashtag (Community-Owned)

**#SidelinePapi**

This is not a campaign hashtag. This is the address. Every dad who tags a photo from the sideline with #SidelinePapi is adding to the archive — a living document of what it looks like to be this kind of father, in this sport, on these fields, across every city and climate and circumstance. It becomes the gathering point because it is simple enough to remember mid-Saturday-morning when a dad wants to share something real and broad enough to belong to any dad on any sideline, not just followers of the account. The goal by Week 10 is for user-generated posts to start appearing under this tag without prompting.

---

### Secondary Set (Consistent on Most Posts)

**#YouthSoccer** — The primary reach hashtag. High volume, broad discovery. Every youth soccer parent in the country searches this tag at some point. This is the front door.

**#SoccerDad** — Direct community identification. Dads already self-apply this tag. Showing up in this hashtag's feed puts Sideline Papi in front of the exact audience without any algorithm translation needed.

**#KidsWhoPlay** — A niche-adjacent tag that bridges sports parents to the broader parenting-content conversation without leaning into generic parenting hashtag territory.

**#FieldDad** — Lower volume, higher intent. The dads who use this tag are already showing up with their phones at the field. These are early adopters — the ones most likely to follow, engage, and eventually represent a local chapter.

**#MorningGameDay** — Captures the Saturday-morning ritual specifically. Discovery tag for the pre-dawn, coffee-in-hand, where-is-the-shin-guard content. Seasonal spike on weekend mornings.

**#ShowUpCulture** — This is the brand pillar made searchable. Lower external traffic, high brand coherence. Every post with this tag reinforces the internal language of the community.

---

### Cultural Hashtags (Spanish-Language and Spanglish)

**#PapiEnLaCancha** — Attaches to all Spanish-led posts and La Cultura en la Cancha pillar content. This is the phrase these dads actually are. It signals cultural specificity without requiring any explanation.

**#Orgullo** — Used selectively, on the bilingual emotional-weight posts only. Not on every post. Overuse kills it. When it appears, it means the post is doing the deeper work.

**#FamiliaPrimero** — Attaches to the community pillar and the monthly dad portrait posts. Broader cultural resonance, bridges the soccer-specific content to the larger Latino family values conversation.

**#ConGanas** — The phrase that already lives in the captions. Making it a hashtag creates a second life for the content — other parents who recognize the phrase will find the post through the tag. This one is an inside signal.

**#ElPapi** — Playful, identity-forward, Spanglish-native. Used on the humor pillar content and the lighter sideline-behavior posts. Lower volume, high cultural recognition.

---

### Hashtags to Avoid

**#DadLife** — Saturated beyond recovery. The tag contains everything from golf content to dad-joke accounts to lifestyle brand advertising. Sideline Papi appearing in that stream signals nothing and costs attention. The brand is specific; this tag is the opposite of specific.

**#SoccerMom** — The obvious note is that it does not describe the audience, but the deeper problem is that using it signals that the brand does not understand its own space. Even as a reach play, it puts content in front of an audience who will not convert and tells the actual audience that the account is not paying attention.

**#Blessed** — This word is the enemy of Sideline Papi's voice. "Blessed" is what you say when you want to perform gratitude without actually being specific about what you're grateful for. Sideline Papi's entire posture is the opposite: name the thing, hold it up, mean it. This hashtag signals the kind of content the brand explicitly is not.

## 7. PLATFORM DIFFERENTIATION

---

**Instagram paragraph:**

Instagram is where Sideline Papi builds its permanent record. The audience here has opted into the brand — they followed, they stayed, they come back — and that means the content can demand more of them: longer captions, slower emotional builds, Spanish-led posts that do not explain themselves. The voice on Instagram is the same voice as TikTok but turned slightly inward, speaking to people who already know the Saturday morning feeling rather than trying to convince new arrivals that it exists. Success on Instagram does not look like virality; it looks like saves, like comments that are full paragraphs, like a DM from a dad in Phoenix saying he read the caption twice. The grid is the brand's architecture — the dad portraits, the community roundups, the quieter bilingual posts — and it should feel, at a scroll distance, like something worth stopping for. Instagram is where the community decides to trust Sideline Papi, and that trust is built post by post, not by any single moment of performance.

**TikTok paragraph:**

TikTok is where Sideline Papi finds people who do not know they are looking for it yet. The audience here has not opted in; they are mid-scroll, mid-snack, and they will be convinced in three seconds or not at all — which means the hook cannot be approximate and the content cannot ask for patience it has not earned. The voice on TikTok leans slightly more English-accessible without losing the Spanglish coding, because the cultural moment needs a breath of entry before it can go deep; the bilingual content still lands, but it earns its way in through humor or recognition first. Success on TikTok for Sideline Papi looks like comments from first-time visitors who say "I don't even have a kid in soccer but I watched this four times," because that comment means the specific has hit the universal — which is exactly what this brand is built to do. TikTok does not build the community; it recruits for it. The dad who laughs at the referee video on Tuesday follows the Instagram on Wednesday and watches the dad portrait on Sunday. The platforms are one funnel, not two audiences.
